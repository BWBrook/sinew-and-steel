# AI as Custodian

This is the worst an AI Custodian will ever be, and it is already useful.

That sentence is meant to date. The claim underneath is plainer: the practical threshold has already been crossed. "GM-less group" and "solo player" no longer require elaborate oracle engines before the table feels alive. A capable model can already do much of the Custodian's work: frame a living world, answer free choices, and make consequences stick.

Sinew & Steel was written with that possibility in mind, but AI is optional. This book is a complete tabletop RPG without it. Think of AI Custodian play as another way to use the same rules, not a different game.

## Full Custodian, not a novelty

You can put an AI to work as a co-Custodian, assistant, brainstorm partner, or NPC voice generator. All of those uses are fine. The use that matters most here is the plain one:

**treat the AI as the full Custodian.**

Not as a toy. Not as a rules vending machine. Not as a text generator you constantly override.

If you want the most S&S-like experience, bring the same social contract you would bring to a human Custodian:

- respect the tone of the table,
- accept consequences without turning every ruling into a negotiation,
- and assume good faith while correcting drift plainly.

The AI is not infallible; neither are people. What matters is momentum, coherence, and trust in the shared fiction.

## What AIs do unusually well

An AI Custodian has some real strengths:

- **Scene craft.** It can put a place on the table quickly: torch-smoke, wet stone, a door that will not quite close.
- **Live options.** It can offer 2-4 plausible paths forward without pretending those are the only possible moves.
- **Character voice.** Given a clear frame, it can keep an NPC's manner, grievance, and appetite in view.
- **Availability.** It will run a scene at midnight, after work, or in the small gap where a human table would never assemble.

## What AIs still do badly (and what to do about it)

AI weaknesses are real, and pretending otherwise makes sessions worse. Most of them are predictable enough to manage:

- **Over-rolling.** Left alone, an AI may gamify everything into checks. The fix is the S&S stance: do not roll by default. Ask for intent, method, and stakes, then roll only if uncertainty matters.
- **Accidental railroading.** An AI may "help" by pulling everything back to a tidy plot. Correct it the same way you would with a human Custodian: restate intent, restate what is true, and ask for options that split in different directions.
- **Context drift.** Even strong models forget details. Give the AI a spine of memory: a current sheet, a short recap, and a last checkpoint. It will become far more consistent.
- **Dice integrity.** Treat dice as external truth: roll physically or with a trusted roller, and tell the AI the results.

## Secrets need a screen

There is one hard truth worth stating plainly: if players can see everything the AI says, secrets cannot exist in the same channel.

That is not a moral problem; it is just information flow. If you want hidden motives, unseen clocks, or private scenario notes, give the AI a private Custodian channel (or keep secrets in human notes) and share only player-safe narration and options.

## Why Sinew & Steel fits AI play

Some RPGs are hard for an AI to run because the rules are exception-heavy: too many bespoke subsystems, too many small modifiers, too many places for drift to creep in.

S&S leans the other way by design: one core resolution language, a small number of tracked resources, and consequences that can be narrated cleanly. That makes it easier for a model to stay consistent, and easier for a human table to correct it when it wobbles.

## Three ways to use an AI

Three common "AI Custodian" use cases work well:

- **Solo play:** the AI is the world, and you are the party.
- **GM-less groups:** the AI runs the session so no one has to shoulder the GM role.
- **Busy Custodians:** the AI is a co-pilot: generating options, voicing NPCs, and keeping momentum when your energy is low.

Pick the one that fits your life. The rules do not care.
