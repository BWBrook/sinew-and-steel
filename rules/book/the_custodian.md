# The Custodian

Not the author of a pre-written plot. Not the opponent. Not a safety rail that stops anything bad from happening.

Your job in Sinew & Steel is social, not mechanical: **maintain momentum, narrate consequences, and adjudicate fairly** — with an eye on player fun even when it means delivering outcomes they don’t *want*.

## Momentum and real choice

I like Custodian play that offers alternatives without turning them into narrow gates.

Players should feel free to surprise you. You shouldn’t need to protect “your plan”, because ideally you don’t have one in the first place — only a situation, a few pressures, and a sense of what the world wants.

When a player hesitates or asks “what are my options?”, the most useful response is rarely a single instruction. It’s usually:

- 2–4 plausible options (and an explicit “or anything else that makes sense”), and
- enough scene clarity that they can choose with confidence.

“Real player choice” is not a slogan. It’s a practical promise: different decisions should lead to genuinely different futures, and you shouldn’t quietly steer everything back to a pre-determined plot.

**Real choice:** “Do we bargain with the rival hunters, steal from them, or offer them a share — knowing that each choice changes who hates us, who trusts us, and what happens when winter gets worse?”

**Fake choice:** “Pick one of three paths through the forest” when all three paths converge back to the same prepared scene, with the same outcome, only wearing a different hat.

Let it branch.

## Telegraph danger like the real world does

I’m not interested in “balanced encounters” or heroic plot armour. The world is what it is: dangerous, uneven, and indifferent.

But indifference is not the same as gotchas. When something is lethal, unstable, or wildly above the party’s weight, telegraph it **narratively and subtly** — enough that attentive players can read it, ask sensible questions, and adapt. A good sting is reality catching up; a bad sting is contrived.

The goal is not to protect players from risk. The goal is to make risk *legible* so their choices stay real.

## Don’t roll by default (Custodian edition)

S&S gets worse if you gamify everything into checks. Roll when uncertainty meets meaningful stakes.

I resolve these narratively more often than not:

- most conversational roleplay (let people talk; roll only when it truly matters),
- low-risk tasks with no time pressure (if repetition is plausible, success is often cleanest),
- trivial group efforts (don’t pretend “three people heaving” is uncertain).

When you do call for a roll, keep it to one meaningful roll, not a ladder of micro-checks.

## Failure is a toolkit, not a chain

There is no baked-in “failure always means X” sequence in S&S. That would get predictable, and predictability kills tension.

Your consequence toolkit includes (at least): Pressure rises, time/position shifts, resources drain, harm lands, threats escalate, clocks tick, and scenes change shape.

Choose the consequence that best fits the fiction, the tone, and the moment.

If nothing obvious fits, make it explicit and fair:

- offer two plausible consequences and pick one openly (a d6 is fine), or
- ask the table which consequence feels most honest for the scene.

## Prep-light, situation-first

My preferred Custodian prep is low and local.

In practice, “little to no prep” looks like this:

- Know the PCs (stats and current state).
- Know what is true *right now* (the immediate situation and what’s about to happen next).
- Know what wants something (one or two forces with simple motives), and what happens if the PCs do nothing.

Even with a written module, you shouldn’t need to read it cover-to-cover to run it. You need the next beat and the forces in motion. The rest can be found at the table.

## Rulings when rules are silent

When the rules don’t answer a question cleanly: make a judgement (briefly explained) or roll openly between a couple of plausible outcomes. Either way, keep the fiction coherent and the table moving.

## Indecision is still a choice

You don’t need to punish players for thinking. But if indecision becomes stalling, treat time as real.

Gentle “time passes” techniques that keep agency intact:

- the tension fuse quietly burns (Pressure rises, a clock ticks),
- the world changes (patrols return, rooms repopulate, trouble arrives).

Players learn a powerful lesson: doing nothing is still doing something — and the world moves with or without them.

## Combat: behave like a world, not a game

My stance on combat is simple: make it realistic, in the sense of *motivated*.

Animals want food and safety. People want status, revenge, coin, or survival. Most opponents don’t want a fair fight — and many will retreat if the cost stops making sense. This keeps the world feeling real and gives players more ways to win: by driving something off, bargaining, bribing, intimidating, outsmarting, or escaping.

## What I avoid (without moralising)

These are my personal red flags as a Custodian:

- Over-warning (which often turns into railroading).
- Fudging dice or quietly protecting the party with plot armour.
- Sacrificing world fidelity just to keep the heroes feeling heroic.

If you want a heroic game, you can absolutely run one — but I’d rather it be heroic because the players were brave in a world that didn’t have to be kind.

## A vignette: the cost of hesitation

> The party has the map, the torch, and the door half-open.  
> They argue for three minutes about whether to go left or right.  
> I don’t interrupt. I just describe the torch sputtering and the distant scrape of stone on stone.  
> When they finally choose, I say, “Good. Mark time passing.”  
> Later, when they return to the entry chamber, it isn’t empty anymore.  
> They don’t feel punished. They feel the dungeon breathing.

That’s S&S Custodian play as I imagine it: fair, sharp, and alive.
