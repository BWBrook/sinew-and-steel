# Preface

I wrote **Sinew & Steel** because I kept having the same experience with role-playing games I otherwise loved: the *idea* of the adventure was always lighter than the *weight* of the system.

Some games buried the table in rule volume. Others were mechanically elegant but drifted under pressure: one GM’s “that seems fair” became another GM’s “no, the system doesn’t allow that”, and the game slowly turned into a pile of exceptions. Even in old-school play, where the spirit was sharp and the nights were memorable, the logic was often janky: awkward saving throws, strange gaps, and a feeling that the rules were historic artefacts rather than a coherent engine.

My first RPG experiences weren’t massive hardbacks. They were gamebooks, solo adventures, and boxed-set play that got you moving quickly. You made decisions, you took risks, you bled for them, and the world *remembered* what happened. That balance — *fast to start, infinite to extend* — is what I wanted again, but without the illogic and clutter.

So this is my attempt to set down a lean design frame: a chassis with **intuitive, consistent mechanics** that lets players and Custodians focus on controlled narrative play *without drift*.

Not the “right” way to play RPGs. Just the way I like them most.

## What this game is trying to do

Sinew & Steel is built to make a few promises and keep them:

- **One roll resolves the moment, not the minute.** You don’t roll to tie your boots. You roll when uncertainty meets meaningful stakes, and then the fiction moves.
- **Every point matters, and the odds stay transparent.** If you raise a stat by one, you feel it. If you spend a resource now, you understand what you’re giving up later.
- **Consequences are visible and tracked.** The game doesn’t just say “that was stressful”. It gives you a tension fuse you can watch climb until it finally blows.
- **Custom characters, real trade-offs — without build optimisation culture.** You can tune a character, but the system resists the “solved build” mentality and keeps the numbers legible.
- **You can re-project a lot of RPG ideas onto this chassis without inventing new dice or special cases.** Different genres get different skins, but the underlying logic remains the same.

Underneath it all, S&S aims for momentum: a conversation-first table style where rulings are quick, stakes are clear, and mechanics appear exactly when they should — not constantly, and not never.

## What this game is *not*

It’s worth saying plainly what I designed against:

- If you want **crunchy, bonus-driven play** where stacking situational modifiers is the core fun, you’ll find S&S deliberately uncooperative.
- If you prefer **exception-based design** (hundreds of bespoke sub-systems and special-case rules that override the core), S&S will feel too restrained.
- If you want **a heavily-prescribed setting bible** that answers every lore question in advance, S&S will not provide that bulk. Skins set the frame; your table fills in the world.

None of this is a judgement. Plenty of people love those styles, and they produce great campaigns. I simply don’t, and I built this to suit my taste: coherent, legible, and prep-light.

## Why these specific choices?

You’ll see a few recurring design instincts throughout the book.

**Roll-under d20 with margin** makes ability feel direct: you don’t translate a stat into a bonus and then hope the bonus matters. You have a target number you can see, and your closeness to it *means something*. That “margin” information is already present in the roll — I wanted to use it to add narrative nuance rather than turning the outcome into a binary pass/fail at an arbitrary line.

**Advantage/Disadvantage** exists because it’s one of the rare modern mechanics that adds texture without adding bloat. I like mechanics you can remember without looking them up. I like mechanics that are hard to abuse. And I like mechanics that work as well for opposed tests as they do for simple checks.

**Luck as both a stat and a spendable pool** is here for a very specific reason: it forces a trade-off between the now and the future. Spend Luck to twist a moment in your favour, and you’ve changed what the next “pure fortune” test feels like. It also makes sense in the fiction: fortune might always have a chance to smile, but you can’t demand it endlessly without cost.

**Pressure as a short fuse** exists to formalise the thing that often floats untracked in RPG play: accumulating strain. You can push, you can gamble, you can keep going — but you cannot do it forever without the boiler eventually blowing. That creates a clear rhythm: rising tension, a crisis, then a reset — without needing a GM to “remember to be harsh”.

## Why skins?

I didn’t want a system that only sings in one genre. I also didn’t want ten separate rulebooks. Skins are my compromise: small, setting-forward overlays that change what the tension *means*, what the stats *represent*, and what kinds of consequences fit the fiction — without changing the underlying engine.

If you’ve played widely, you’ll notice that several skins are love letters to specific traditions: dungeon-crawl austerity, fogbound horror, blue-book space commerce, wanderer fantasy elegy, and so on. I keep the nods subtle on purpose. The goal isn’t to recreate anyone else’s work. The goal is to capture a *play cadence* — the feel of the genre — on a coherent chassis.

And if you want to build your own: the Customisation chapter is here to help you do it without accidentally reinventing a second game inside the first.

## How to use this book (a practical reading path)

If you want to play tonight:

1. Read the **Quickstart**.
2. Read the **Clanfire** skin (it’s the closest to “pure core”).
3. Run the **Emberfall** scenario.

That’s enough. Truly.

If you want to go deeper:

- Players: read **The Adventurer** preamble, then the **Adventurer’s Manual**.
- Custodians: read **The Custodian** preamble, then the **Custodian’s Almanac**.
- When you want different genres, pick a skin that matches your mood (or work through them in order of complexity).

## A note on AI (optional)

Sinew & Steel is designed so a reasoning engine can run it consistently — which makes it unusually friendly to solo play and “GM-less” groups. AI is not required, and nothing in the core rules depends on it. But if you’re curious, the AI appendices are written as practical guidance: how to keep secrets, how to prevent over-rolling, and how to make sure player choice remains real.

## An invitation

Everything here is deliberately carved lean. That’s not because I think complexity is evil; it’s because I think *bloat* is. The chassis is coherent, and coherence is fragile — but it’s also hackable. House-rule freely. Combine skins. Write your own. Just do it with intention, and keep an eye on what you’re trading away when you add weight.

If this book gives you a system that gets out of the way until it matters — and then matters in a way you can feel — it has done its job.
