# Preface

I wrote *Sinew & Steel* after a familiar frustration with role-playing games I otherwise loved: the adventure in my head often felt lighter than the system built to carry it.

Some games buried the table in rules. Others were elegant until play put pressure on them: one GM’s “that seems fair” became another’s “the system doesn’t allow that”, and exceptions piled up. Even old-school play, sharp and memorable as it was, often carried janky logic: awkward saves, punishing randomness, strange gaps, and rules that felt like historic artefacts more than a coherent game engine.

My first RPG experiences came in gamebooks, solo adventures, and boxed sets that got you moving quickly. You made decisions, took risks, bled for them, and the fictional worlds *remembered* what happened. I wanted that balance again: *fast to start and infinite to extend*, with the old illogic and clutter pared away.

So this book sets down the frame I wanted: a lean chassis with intuitive, consistent mechanics, visible odds, and enough structure to let the table improvise without the game wobbling.

This is simply the way I like RPGs most.

## What this game is trying to do

Sinew & Steel is built to make a few promises and keep them:

- One roll resolves the decisive moment. Dice enter when uncertainty meets meaningful stakes, and then the fiction moves.
- Every point matters, and the odds stay transparent. If you raise a stat by one, you feel it. If you spend a resource now, you understand what you’re giving up later.
- Consequences are visible and tracked. The game gives you a tension fuse you can watch climb until it finally blows.
- Custom characters, real trade-offs. Every strength leaves a cost somewhere else.
- A flexible chassis. Different genres get different skins, but the underlying logic remains the same.

Underneath it all, S&S wants momentum: table talk first, clear stakes, and dice reserved for moments they can sharpen.

## A narrower taste

S&S follows a particular set of tastes:

- Fictional positioning carries the weight that crunchy games give to stacked situational modifiers.
- One coherent engine takes the place of bespoke sub-systems and special-case rules.
- Skins set the frame; your table fills in the world instead of consulting a complete setting bible.

Those other styles produce great campaigns for plenty of people. This book is for a narrower taste: a game that stays legible and asks for little prep.

## Why these specific choices?

You’ll see a few recurring design instincts throughout the book.

*Roll-under d20 with margin* makes ability feel direct. Your stat is the target number, and your closeness to it *means something*. The roll already contains that margin, so S&S can spend it on narrative nuance beyond a hard pass/fail line.

*Advantage/Disadvantage* adds texture while keeping the procedure light. It is easy to remember, hard to abuse, and works as well for opposed tests as it does for simple checks.

*Luck as score and spendable pool* forces a trade-off between the present and the future. Spend Luck to twist a moment in your favour, and you’ve changed what the next “pure fortune” test feels like. It also makes sense in the fiction: fortune might always have a chance to smile, but each demand depletes your reserve.

*Pressure as a short fuse* formalises something that often floats untracked in RPG play: accumulating strain. You can push, gamble, and keep going until Pressure reaches crisis. The result is a clear rhythm: rising tension, crisis, reset. The game remembers the strain for the Custodian.

## Why skins?

Skins are my compromise between one system that sings in many genres and ten separate rulebooks. These small, setting-forward overlays change what the tension *means*, what the stats *represent*, and what kinds of consequences fit the fiction while preserving the underlying engine.

If you’ve played widely, you’ll notice that several skins are love letters to specific traditions: dungeon-crawl austerity, fogbound horror, blue-book space commerce, wanderer fantasy elegy, and so on. I keep the nods subtle on purpose. Each one captures a *play cadence*, the feel of the genre, with the same dice under it.

And if you want to build your own, the Customisation chapter will help you keep the original engine intact.

## How to use this book (a practical reading path)

If you want to play tonight:

1. Read the Quickstart.
2. Read the Clanfire skin (it’s the closest to “pure core”).
3. Run the Emberfall scenario.

That’s enough. Truly!

If you want to go deeper:

- Players: read The Adventurer preamble, then the Adventurer’s Manual.
- Custodians: read The Custodian preamble, then the Custodian’s Almanac.
- When you want different genres, pick a skin that matches your mood (or work through them in order of complexity: that's how they're arranged).

## A note on AI GMs (optional)

Sinew & Steel is designed so an AI reasoning engine can run it consistently, which makes it unusually friendly to solo play and “GM-less” groups. The complete tabletop game stands on its own. For curious readers, the AI for Solo Play section covers the practical bits: keeping secrets, avoiding over-rolls, and making sure player choice remains real. The repo also includes a free harness an AI agent can use to run S&S, a bit like a light RPG simulator.

## An invitation

Everything here is deliberately carved lean because *bloat* adds dead weight. The chassis is coherent, and coherence is fragile, but it is also hackable. House-rule freely. Combine skins. Write your own. Just do it with intention, and keep an eye on what you trade away when you add weight.

If this book gives you a system that gets out of the way until it matters, and then matters in a way you can feel, it has done its job.
