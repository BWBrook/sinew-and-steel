# Customisation

Sinew & Steel is deliberately lean, which means it takes customisation unusually well — and unusually badly.

Add the right kind of weight and you get texture: a sharper genre, a stronger tone, a new kind of tension.  
Add the wrong kind and you don’t get depth — you get drag.

My guiding principle for homebrew in this system is simple:

> **If it doesn’t create a decision or generate momentum, it’s clutter.**

It’s the trade I made when I chose a small chassis: every extra moving part has to earn its place.

## Skins are optional

This is worth stating plainly: in S&S, a **skin is not required**.

You can run pure core play with five generic attributes, Luck, Stamina, and Pressure, and let your campaign discover its own voice as you play.

Skins exist to do two things:

1. Rename the core so it feels natural in a genre.
2. Add a small number of procedures that the genre genuinely needs.

## What a skin actually is

In S&S terms, a skin is a **framing layer**. It changes flavour and emphasis without rewriting the engine.

A skin might:

- rename the five attributes (and decide which one is Luck),
- rename Pressure and describe what a crisis looks like,
- add one or two genre procedures.

But a skin should not be a second game hiding inside the first.

## The invariants (what I try not to break)

If you want your custom work to still feel like Sinew & Steel, keep these intact:

- **Resolution stays d20 roll-under**, and margin matters.
- The chassis is still **five attributes + Luck tokens + Stamina + Pressure (0–5)**.
- **Pressure** is a short fuse: when it hits 5, something breaks, then it resets.
- **Advantage/Disadvantage** remains the primary way fictional positioning changes the odds.
- **No additive modifier stacking** as a new normal.
- **If you need a new dice type, you’re probably solving the wrong problem.**

You can bend these at your private table, of course. But if you bend too many, you’re no longer reskinning S&S — you’re designing a new system.

## The minimum a skin should define

If you write a new skin, try to make it runnable by someone who has never played your genre before.

At minimum, I want a skin to answer:

- **What stories is this for?** (One paragraph. Scope prevents drift.)
- **The five attributes** (keys + names + what they cover), including which one is Luck.
- **What Pressure is called here**, what it means, and what a crisis looks like.
- **How recovery tends to work** in this genre (especially Luck refresh).
- **Any special procedures** (written in core terms: when to roll, what changes, what costs tick).

If you do those five things cleanly, most of the rest is optional seasoning.

## Safe ways to add texture (things I actively encourage)

If you want your skin to feel distinct without becoming complicated, these moves are reliable:

- Rename core terms so they match the genre’s instincts.
- Declare typical costs in-fiction (“magic usually costs Pressure”, “gunfights spike Pressure quickly”).
- Add story-facing clocks for long tension (patrols, storms, suspicion) when it helps.
- Give concrete examples of Advantage/Disadvantage in the genre (home turf, leverage, ritual prep, cover).
- Add gear and NPCs using existing tags (edge, soak, and simple tier + Stamina statlines) instead of inventing new numeric layers.

The skins in this book show the range of what’s possible — from light overlays to heavier genre modules.

## Red flags (things that usually cause drift)

These are the moves that tend to bloat the game or distort the feel:

- The skin implies constant rolling (“roll to travel”, “roll every interaction”) instead of using stakes and uncertainty.
- You start stacking situational bonuses (“+1 here, +2 there”) instead of using fictional positioning and Advantage/Disadvantage.
- A “helpful” skill list quietly becomes prescriptive (“always roll X for Y”), and suddenly the character sheet is a menu.
- You add a second universal meta-track that behaves like Pressure in every scene (unless it’s clearly optional).
- The skin changes what success means (“success still costs you most of the time”) without naming it as a deliberate genre dial.

## Tags, not skill lists

If you want the feeling of “skills” without turning the game into a catalogue of niche rolls, use **tags**: small, named traits that grant a specific kind of edge.

Keep them narrow, contextual, and sometimes limited to once per session so they stay punchy.

Examples (in the spirit of the pregens in this book):

- *Streetwise:* once per session, Advantage on a test when dealing with criminals or dock-bosses.

This gives players identity and competence without turning your table into “which of my 27 skills applies here?”

## Mixing skins (without overload)

If you want to borrow rules from multiple skins, my preferred method is:

1. **Pick one attribute set.** That’s the language your table speaks.
2. **Pick one Pressure track.** That’s the fuse that gives the campaign its rhythm.
3. If multiple skins add extra resources, **choose one**.
4. Remap everything else onto your chosen core: rename where needed, and reframe costs so they make sense.

## Writing new rules (my “8–12 lines” test)

Keep new procedures small enough to be remembered. My favourite constraint is: **can you write it in 8–12 lines without losing clarity?**

A good S&S procedure usually has this shape:

- when it triggers, what you roll, what success changes, what failure changes, and what cost ticks (if any).

If you feel a new subsystem pulling you toward new dice, nested tables, or a page of edge cases, step back and ask: what decision is this meant to create, and what momentum is it meant to generate? If you can’t answer, it’s probably clutter.
