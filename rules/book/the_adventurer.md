# The Adventurer

Sinew & Steel asks something simple of you as a player: **make coherent narrative choices, and commit to them.**

It’s a roleplaying game, not a life simulator. The question isn’t “what would I do?” but “what would *this person* do, right now, with their courage, habits, pride, and fear?”

The rules are lean on purpose: they’re meant to catch you when stakes matter, then get out of the way so the story keeps momentum.

## Play the person, not the puzzle

You can tune a character in S&S, but it’s not built for “beating the game” through build optimisation, rules lawyering, or fishing for checks without narrative motivation. Those habits flatten play.

The better posture is: **play to find out what your character becomes** — and accept that failure is part of the fun, not a mistake to avoid at all costs.

## Momentum is a resource

When S&S sings, it feels like it’s happening in real time. Do your part to keep that pace:

- Be ready to act when the spotlight lands on you.
- Choose an approach quickly, even if it isn’t perfect.
- If you hesitate, treat it as real in the fiction: time passes, attention shifts, danger closes.

This isn’t a demand for reckless play. It’s an invitation to keep the night moving.

## Declare intent, method, risk

The most useful sentence shape in S&S is:

- **I want** ___ (intent)  
- **by doing** ___ (method)  
- **and I’m worried** ___ (risk)

Example:

> “I want the guard looking the other way, so I distract him with a joke and a quick question — but I’m worried he recognises me.”

Give the Custodian something vivid and coherent, and you’ll often get something in return: a clean narrative outcome, Advantage, or a failure cost that feels fair.

## Don’t interrogate the future

In some games it’s normal to ask “what happens if I fail?” before rolling. In S&S, that can become a soft cheat: it turns uncertainty into negotiation, and it invites railroading.

Try this instead:

- Ask in-fiction questions (“How thick is the ice?” “Do I smell smoke?”).
- Ask for clarity of situation, not certainty of outcome.
- Or commit and accept that you don’t get to see around corners.

Trust the Custodian to adjudicate in good faith.

## Let stats follow approach

By default, the Custodian calls the stat. But you may propose an alternative if it matches your approach better — with a one-line justification.

If there’s disagreement, the Custodian decides, briefly and without drama. (Nothing kills momentum like turning a tense beat into a courtroom.)

## Sometimes you don’t roll at all

You can earn roll-free outcomes by removing uncertainty: good prep, good tools, good positioning, clever plans, or an approach that makes success plausible without contest. This isn’t “getting away with it”; it’s the game rewarding coherent choices.

## Spend Luck like a storyteller

Luck tokens are a trade-off between the now and the later. Two good instincts coexist:

- Spend early to seize momentum (the roll that defines the scene).
- Save for existential risk (the roll that might end the adventure).

When you nudge a die, narrate it. Don’t say “I spend a token”; say what it looks like: a lucky foothold, a surge of courage, a remembered detail, a coincidence that feels like destiny — or (in some skins) a price paid to something listening.

## Failure is fuel

Failure in S&S can mean a hard stop, a complication, progress at a cost, or success with collateral damage. All are valid.

If you never fail, you’re probably not taking risks — and risk is where adventure lives. But “embrace failure” is not permission to sabotage the table: your character should still want to live, and your choices should still respect everyone’s fun.

## Fighting is a kind of scene (not a points system)

Combat is dangerous, and “victory” means what the fiction says it means. Good instincts:

- Avoid fair fights; make them unfair in your favour.
- Retreat is honourable; survival is a win condition.
- Threats, bargains, bribes, ambushes, and misdirection are tactics.
- Driving something off can be as decisive as killing it.

## Table habits that help

- Share the spotlight; pull another character into your plan.
- Say what you spend (Luck, Stamina, Pressure) without hiding it like a gotcha.
- Accept consequences without argument.
- Stay engaged; be ready when your moment arrives.

## A tiny vignette: choosing danger on purpose (Iron & Ruin)

In **Iron & Ruin**, sorcery is a bargain, not “free damage”.

> The cultists are closing and the iron gate won’t budge.  
> “You can try to lift it,” the Custodian says, “but not before they reach you.”  
> The sorceress smiles. “Then I *Rend* it.”  
> “Wrack-tier. Test Will. No Fortune nudges. Doom rises even on success.”  
> She doesn’t ask what failure looks like. She speaks the forbidden name anyway.  
> The gate screams, warps and splits — the party bolts through as the air turns cold. 
> She wipes the blood from her lip, marks +1 Doom, and laughs, thrilled by the risk.

That’s the point: you adventure because it’s dangerous, because it matters, and because the story gets richer when you choose the hard path with open eyes.
